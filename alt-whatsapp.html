Here are the Alternatives for Whatsapp Messenger

WhatsApp, owned by Facebook, now has more than 2.5 billion users worldwide and is the biggest online messenger app on the market. Founded in 2009 by ex-Yahoo employees it started as a small startup and swelled to 250,000 users in just a few months, growing so fast that they had to add a charge for using the service per year to slow the subscription rate down. In 2014, WhatsApp was acquired by Facebook and has seen continued growth, reaching the 1 billion mark in July 2017.

WhatsApp has been sharing data with Facebook since 2016, but users were able to opt out and still use the app. That will no longer be possible after February 8. the new privacy policy has left many unnerved as data privacy has become a significant cause of concern. WhatsApp announced its new data privacy policy in early February and prompted the users to comply with it by February 8, 2021,

Fortunately, there are a handful of alternative messaging apps out there if you value your privacy more than convenience. Here’s our pick of the best. Most of the platforms and apps in the market, such as Signal, Telegram, Discord allow a user to access them while maintaining their anonymity at the same time. Here are a few of the best alternatives for WhatsApp messenger.

1. Signal

Signal has been around for a few years, first launching in 2014. But it’s only really been since the George Floyd and Black Lives Matter demonstrations of summer 2020 that the app has gained ground. One of the app’s first major proponents in 2020 was Jack Dorsey, the CEO of Twitter who recommended users download Signal in response to heightened awareness of police monitoring of other communication-based apps.

Signal has become one of the most popular messaging apps around. Favored by tech giants, cybersecurity experts, journalists, government officials, and many more, Signal has gone from a darling secret of the security community to something even the non-tech savvy are using. Signal can do pretty much everything that WhatsApp can. It uses your phone’s data connection to send end-to-end encrypted messages – or voice notes, images, or videos – to individuals and groups. The app uses its own open-source, end-to-end encryption protocol and, as a bonus, is owned by a non-profit foundation rather than a tech giant like Facebook (WhatsApp's parent company).

2. Telegram

Telegram is a multi-platform messaging service founded by Russian entrepreneur Pavel Durov. It first rolled out on iOS and Android in late 2013, and now has an estimated 500 million monthly users. Telegram may be among the most popular messaging services in the world, yet there are still many who don’t know what it’s all about.

Telegram is one of the best encrypted messaging apps, offering a cross-platform, cloud-based distributed messaging app that has no ads. It’s similar to Apple’s Messages, but it’s more reliable, has dedicated apps for all platforms — PC, Mac, Linux, Android, and iOS — and it also allows you to set up secret chats with messages that can self-destruct, giving you a new level of privacy. What makes Telegram unique is its focus on privacy, encryption, and an open-source API. There are countless unofficial clients to go along with the official Telegram apps and web interface. It also allows multiple devices to use the same account (verified by SMS), and multiple accounts on the same device.

3. Viber

Viber was initially launched for iPhone on December 2, 2010, in direct competition with Skype. It was launched on the Android platform on July 19, 2012, followed by BlackBerry and Windows Phone on May 8, 2012, and Nokia's Series 40, Symbian, and Samsung's Bada platform on July 24, 2012, by which time the application had 90 million users. right now Viber has more than 1.1 billion users.

This is yet another privacy messenger app, which supports end-to-end encryption. All types of messages, photos, videos, voice and video calls, and group chats are encrypted. You can use the free app for text messages, voice calls, and video calls. Unlike some messaging apps, Viber doesn't require a username and password. Instead, after installing the app, it relies on your phone number to identify you, so users can't disguise their identity behind a screen name, alias, or avatar.
